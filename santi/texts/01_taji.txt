Apparatus
The sigla Br and Na refer to Brandes’ and Nakada’s editions, respectively. In view of the fact that the fifth section was read by Kern and Nakada, both of them yielding rather different readings, for this part I use the siglum K, which stands for Kern.
<app>1.1 śrī sañjaya-varsā⟨tīta⟩ ◇ From the presence of <i>tarung</i> to signify a long <i>ā</i> in <i>varṣā</i> and from the fact that the word <i>sañjayavarṣa</i> is normally followed by <i>atīta</i>,<i></i> becoming <i>sañjayavarṣātīta</i> through <i>sandhi</i>, we infer that the two last syllables of <i>atīta</i> have been involuntarily omitted.</app>

<app>1.17 I sira ⟨⟨piṅi⟩⟩ piṁhay ◇ I sigaḍiri Br Na. Our new reading is based on our understanding of the intended meaning of the text. The previous editions’ reading <i>sigaḍiri</i> seems hard to fit in the context where the <i>sīma</i> is the favor of the Lords to the <i>piṅhay</i>s and <i>vahuta</i>s. Besides, the combination of “<i>I sira</i>” occurs frequently in the text, namely in lines 1.5, 1.10, and 1.23. Given the clear reading of <i>si</i> and a space that can accommodate as many as four <i>akṣara</i>s, we suspect that the engraver had erroneously written <i>sira</i> <i>piṁhay</i> as <i>sira piṅi</i>, upon which he canceled <i>piṅi</i> and again wrote <i>piṅhay</i>, so that the syllables <i>piṅi</i> should be treated as superfluous.</app>

<app>1.21 (duvəg·) ◇ (...) Br, juru-juru Na. In none of the visual documentation available to us, this part of the stone can be read with any certainty. The previous reading by Nakada, <i>juru-juru</i>, seems doubtful to us because it does not fit in the context. We at first focused our search for a suitable word on terms expressing the ritual action called <i>uyut</i> or meaning “in front of” or “facing”. Given the space that suffices for about 3–4 <i>akṣara</i>s, we try to advance two possibilites of reading, which both of them yield their own advantage and disadvantage. The first option is to read <i>huvus</i> as in 1.20. It results us two consecutives sentences which both start with <i>huvus</i>. Here, <i>huvus</i> stands as the auxiliary when combined with <i>śinivi</i> that comes after. However, it would nuance the text as if the stone was worshipped in the past (<i>śinivi</i>), whereas the subjects who were worshipped refer to Sanjaya and Balitung. The second option is to have <i>duvəg</i>, which also occurs earlier in 1.14. The meaning would be a bit puzzling and less make sense but it is the least bad since it causes no syntax problem in the sentence.</app>

<app>2.8 (pu) sukha ◇ sa sukha Br, sa(ṁ) sukha Na. Here the difficulty to read what the text intends to say lies in the first <i>akṣara.</i> It can be understood as either <i>sa</i> or <i>pa</i> with a longer downward extension on its left vertical, as if it has a <i>suku</i> in the wrong place. To render it as <i>sasukha</i> is not a good solution since this passage concerns a function which is normally followed by an honorific and name. We have considered the possibility of reading <i>sa</i> and supplying an <i>anusvāra</i>. Indeed, we find that the sequence <i>saṁ sukha</i> occurs in other inscriptions, but <i>sukha</i> is then normally part of the expression <i>sukha paṅgil</i>, as in Wuatan Tija (2r3–4) <i>samgat pinapan samgat savyan samgat sukha paṅgil</i>,<i></i> Poh (1v8–9) <i>rakai halaran· pu tloḍuṁ, samagat· dalinan· saṁ sukha paṁgil</i>. Only in Turyan (A42) does <i>saṁ sukha</i> stand independently. However, the surrounding names in the Turyan context also have the structure <i>saṁ X</i>, while in the present context, the structure <i>pu</i> + proper name is predominant. On these grounds, we prefer to assume that <i>pu</i> was intended but realized with an eccentric shape.</app>

Commentary
1.4 <i>samgət</i> Lua is also attested in Gilikan I (A5) and Panggumulan II ( line 11). In Panggumulan I (2v7), it only writes <i>lua</i> and in Poh (2r14), it appears as <i>saṅ ra</i> Lua. In other contexts, Lua serves as a toponym. Cf. Poh (1v19) Panggumulan I (2v8), Bhatari (1v13), Rukam (1v16) and Lintakan (2r4). In the first four charters, it has <i>ra</i> in front of <i>lua,</i> hence <i>vatak ra lua.</i>
1.4 <i>pu</i> Ḍapit as a native of Pandamuan village, Vadihati district figures several times in the Balitung corpus. Cf. Kayu Ara Hiwang (A9), Rongkab (1r7), and Turu Mangambil (A6)<i>.</i> Whether it still has any connection with the Vadihati district or not, this personage also seems to hold the function of Vadihati, as in Ayam Teas I (1r6), Ayam Teas II (1r4), Rongkab (1r7), Samalagi (1v10), Panggumulan I (1v11), Telang I (1r8), Telang II (1r3), Poh (1v9), Mantyasih I (1r12), Hujung Galuh (1v11), Turu Mangambil (A6), Kaladi (8v6) and Barahasrama (1v6).<i></i> Exceptional notes for this figure: 1) in Kasugihan 829 (1v3), the district is attested differently: <i>vatak ayam təas</i> and apparently his function accords to it, hence <i>saṅ pamgat ayam təas</i>; 2) in Kaladi (8v6), he is addressed as <i>dyah ḍampit</i>, but still hold the function <i>vadihati</i>; 3) in Lintakan, <i>pu</i> Ḍapit goes with a Mamrati function.
It is actually written as <i>ḍaṅpit</i>, but Damais (1970: 364) left a footnote as follows: “forme secondaire de <i>« ḍapit »</i> qui est la forme usuelle. On voit, par cet exemple de 823 Saka<i>,</i> quels phénomènes de nasalisation interne sont anciens<i>.</i>”<i></i> Other spelling variants of this name are <i>dhapit</i> (Hujung Galuh 1v11) and <i>ḍampit</i> (Turu Mangambil A6 and Kaladi 8v6).
1.6 <i>pu</i> Dhanada as <i>saṅ halaran</i> is also attested in the Balitung corpus, namely in Rongkab (1r6 and 1v9), Poh (1v12), Mantyasih I (1r13), Mantyasih III (2r2), Kasugihan (1v4). In all of those occurrences, he is attested as the native of Pandamuan village (the spelling is varied: <i>paṅramvan</i> in Poh 1v12, <i>paṅḍamuan</i> in Mantyasih I 1v13, <i>paramuan</i> in Mantyasih III 2r2). The district is also not always <i>vadihati;</i> some charters have <i>ayam təas</i> instead. Cf. Mantyasih I (1r13), Mantyasih III (2r2) and Kasugihan (1v4–5).
1.8 The combination of toponym <i>gunuṅan</i> and <i>taṅkilan</i> figures as well in Panggumulan I (1v16) and Poh (1v14). In Panggumulan, the suffix -<i>an</i> is dropped, hence only <i>taṅkil.</i> From other inscriptions coming around this period, different villages figure under the district <i>taṅkilan</i>, namely <i>sḍəḥ</i> (Rongkab 1r7), <i>vulakan</i> (Kiringan 1r8) and <i>muṅgu</i> from this inscription (B7–8). In Poh (2r2), however, this toponym serves as a <i>vanua</i>, under the district <i>mamrati.</i>
In the <i>Répertoire onomastique</i>, it is still registered under the old reading version by Brandes, hence <i>muṅga</i> (Damais 1970: 887). A footnote, however, is left by Damais: “peut-être faut-il lire <i>« muṅgu »,</i> toponyme attesté par ailleurs<i>.</i>”<i></i>
1.10 The toponym <i>vaduṅ poh</i> also occurs in Mantyasih I (1r14) and Mantyasih III (2r2). The district (<i>vatak</i>), however, is different. Both figure <i>paṅkur poh</i>, while our charter has <i>makudur</i>. Damais (1970: 677) read it as <i>vaju poh</i> and added this note: “<i>On pourrait être tenté de considérer ce terme comme un erreur pour « vaduṅ poh » mais le « vatak » est différent et il s’agit donc bien d’un autre toponyme.</i>”<i></i>
English translation: “One could be tempted to consider this term as an error for “<i>vaduṅ poh</i>” but the “<i>vatak</i>” is different and it is therefore another toponym.”
1.10 Besides <i>vaduṅ poh</i> and <i>liṅai</i> (A14), the villages under the <i>vatak makudur</i> are as follows<i>: mantyasih</i> (Kayu Ara Hiwang C10, Panggumulan I 1v14, Poh 1v13), <i>puluṅ</i> (Lintakan 1r12).
1.11 The district of Haməas (another spelling: <i>hamyas</i>) is<i></i> also found in Mulak I 1r3–1r4, Kwak I 1r9–1r10, Kwak II 1r2–1r3, Poh 1v12–1v13, Mantyasih I 1v13–1v14, Mantyasih II 1r2–1r3. Under this district, there are several villages: <i>tāl varani</i> (Mulak I 1r3–4, Kwak I 1r10, Kwak II 1r3), <i>kahaṅatan</i> (Poh 1v13, Taji Gunung A12), <i>kataṅguhan</i> (Mantyasih I 1r14), <i>katguhan</i> (Mantyasih III 2r3), <i>ra təguh</i> (Taji Gunung A11). The last three mentioned villages may refer to the same place as indicated by the word <i>təguh</i> that forms the toponyms.
1.11 <i>mandyāṅgin</i> as a personage is attested in Poh (1v13), with an exact origin (village Kahaṅatan, district Hamyas) but bearing a different honorific title: <i>saṅ</i>. As a toponym, it appears in Kinewu (A15: <i>rake maṇḍyāṅin</i>), and Piling-Piling B (A8: <i>rāmanta I maṇḍyaṅin</i>).

1.15 The translation for <i>pu saranna Anak· banuA Iṅ ulu kabikuAn· ri taṁkillan·</i> is given as “pu Sarana, native of the village upstream of the monastery of Taṅkilan”. Here, we encounter an instance of an unnamed village which is specified by its geographical situation in relation to a named monastery (<i>kabikuan</i>). This manner of using religious institutions to specify a place also occurs in several inscriptions. Cf. Palepangan (1v12), Poh (2v11) both of these charters using a named <i>vihāra</i> to specify the villages. According to Griffiths in his article on Buddhist monasteries (forthcoming), this tendency might indicate that the religious foundations had a great social importance at that time.
1.17 Instead of <i>rakryan I sira</i> ⟨⟨<i>piṅi</i>⟩⟩, previous scholars read <i>rakryan I sigaḍiri</i>. The apparent toponym Sigaḍiri does not figure in any Old Javanese source. Rouffaer (1918: 155–57) attempted to analyze this personage, <i>rakryan I sigadiri,</i> by linking this charter with another charter bearing the same Sanjaya era, namely the Timbanan Wungkal stone. He took the Lord of Sigadiri as the protagonist of Taji Gunung, as shown in A17 (<i>sĭma paṅanugraha rakryan· I sigaḍiri</i>), King Daksa as the protagonist of Timbanan Wungkal (A5), and then ventured to conclude that both refer to the same person. Our interpretation, however, goes in a different direction. In view of the fact that <i>sigaḍiri</i> would be attested uniquely in this inscription while the combination <i>I sira</i> figures frequently in this text (and in other inscriptions), we focus on the <i>sīma</i> being a favor of the Lords (<i>rakryan</i>) to the <i>piṅhay</i>s and <i>vahuta</i>s, as seen in the next phrase which mentions the descendant of the concerned officials. Therefore, we expect a sequence which can hark back to the benefactor and the grantee rather than a reading of an unfamiliar name that comes in abruptly and distorts the coherence of the text.

2.21 Kern in VG vol. VII proposes to understand the word <i>paranakan</i> as a class of persons born of a mixture of races or castes (Kern 1917: 47). 4.2 The word <i>pāja</i> that recurs in 4.3 in the form <i>sāpaja</i> is not registered in any Old Javanese dictionary. In view of its function which seems to represent a certain unit of commodity, it might be cognate with another similar word denoting a unit for measuring unhusked rice, i.e<i>. pāda</i> or <i>pada</i>. Cf. Haliwangbang (1v2): <i>saragi pagaṅanan· 1 kampil· 1 vəAs· pada 1,</i> Mamali (1v2): <i>mas· mā 4 vəAs· pada 5</i>, Kwak I (1v8): <i>vḍihan yu 4 bras pāda 1</i>, Kwak II (1r5): <i>raṅga yu 2 vras pada 1</i>, Taragal (1v2): <i>saragi Inuman· 1 kampil· 1 vəAs· pada 1</i>, Taji (3r2): <i>mas· mā 4 vəAs· pada 1</i>, Poh (1v11): <i>mas· su 14 kbo 1 vḍus· 1 pada 1</i>. In fact, in an edition of Rukam issued by Titi Surti Nastiti <i>et al.</i> (1982: 23–28, 36–40), this term actually figures in 2r5: <i>1 kumol· 1 bras· paja 1</i>, but a note is left for it (p. 27 n. 17): “Penulis prasasti terlanjur menulis garis horizontal di tengah, sehingga <i>da</i> menjadi <i>ja</i>.” Consequently in the translation, the word <i>paja</i> is rendered as <i>pada.</i>
