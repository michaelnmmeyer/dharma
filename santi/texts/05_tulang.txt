Tulang Er III (914-12-30)
The first transcription was provided by Trigangga (1987), whose edition was later used by Hery Santosa as starting point for his undergraduate thesis, where this author himself also tried to give alternative readings. The edition below was prepared based on Trigangga’s edition and was checked against the plates directly during my visit to MNI in January 2022.

Translation
(1r1-4) Happiness! Elapsed Śri Sañjaya year 198, month of Poṣya, elevent <i>tithi</i> of the waxing fortnight, Tuṅlai, Umanis, Friday, lunar mansion Kartikā, <i>dahana-devatā,</i> the twenty fourth of the yoga (<i>śukla</i>-<i>yoga</i>). That was the time that the grant of the Great King, the Lord of Hino, Śrī Dakṣottama bāhubajra-pratipaḳsa-ksaya Śrī Mahottuṅgavijayā Viṣṇumurti goes down ……. to the monastery at Tulaṅ Er, Tilimpik district.
(1r4-5) Its cause why they were granted by the Great King is … The Great King arrived at Bulusan, after [passing through] the two forts. The headmen went over to make ready the water for bathing and ….
(1r6) Likewise is their works as the Great King heading to the south. Pu Aya was asked with regard to things that will be granted for him without …
(1r7) shall undertake the wet rice field. Their capabilities to provide the water for bathing in the south for the Great King…
(1r8–9) … their <i>kayā</i>, Two units (<i>alih viji</i>) shall be reduced from them. Then, two <i>tampah</i> of <i>kayā</i> that will be entitled for them. Their words shall be approved by the Great King. Their token of reverences shall reduce of 2 <i>prāṇa</i> of <i>kayā</i>.
(1r9–10) The headmen offer all form of confirmatory gifts that go downwards to the Great King, 10 <i>dhāraṇa</i> of silver ball and for the queen, <i>dyah</i> Buntir, <i>śri prakasatasatya-brati…</i>
(1r11–13) They offer the confirmatory gifts to <i>saṅ</i> .. 1 <i>kāti</i> of silver ball, the <i>tilimpik</i> at that time was <i>pu</i> Ra Hula, native of Karamas…and they offer the confirmatory gifts to the official<i></i> of Baleṅ (called) <i>ḍāṅ ācārya</i> Praṇidhānasupāta, to the official<i></i> of Momah-umah ….<i></i> to <i>pu</i> Ləbvira…, pu Burəl, all were given 1 <i>dhāraṇa</i> of silver ball for each.

Commentary
1r4 The toponym <i>tulaṅ er</i> figures in Tulang Air I (line 4, 19, 27), Tulang Air II (line 4, 20) and Mantyasih III (2r11). In these three charters, the spelling is given as <i>tulaṅ air</i>. Both Tulang Air I and Tulang Air II concern the demarcation of Tulaṅ Air to become a <i>sīma</i>, while in Mantyasih III, <i>tulaṅ air</i> appears among the <i>vanua tpi siriṅ</i> of Mantyasih village. Having two identical toponyms, however, does not mean that they refer to the same place. Tulang Air I, Tulang Air II and Mantyasih III are found in <i>kabupaten</i> Temanggung. The first two inscriptions originated in the vicinity of Candi Perot, while Mantyasih III was found in Ngadirejo. Moreover, the toponyms which are attested in the charters do not show any similarity to those which are attested in Tulang Er plates, hence here we deal with two different places.
1r5 The toponym <i>bulussan</i> is also found in the Balitung corpus, in the Samalagi (1v3) <i>bhaṭāra vaiṣṇava Iṁ bulusan dharmma rakryān mahāmantrī</i>, and Watu Ridang (1r4–5) <i>kabikvan· majuru I bhaṭāra beṣṇava Iṁ bulusan·</i>. Both are spelled with single <i>s</i>. Interestingly, the Samalagi plate is also coming from <i>kabupaten</i> Bantul, precisely in <i>desa</i> Kretek, which is located 10 km in the south of <i>desa</i> Sorok.
Boechari 2012: 478-48, Herni Pramastuti <i>et.al</i> 2015: 92–95.
1r5 The term <i>kuta</i> might refer to a fortified, closed space in the center of the polity or a township. See Wisseman Christie 1999 (p. 243) for her explanation on the Balinese port market that was protected by a fortified <i>kuta</i> from the Balinese epigraphic records during the tenth century CE.
1r8 The term <i>aṇḍǝh</i> in the context of taxation or payment is attested in Rumwiga I (1r8–9) and Kamalagyan (line 5, 6, 16 and 17). Its basic meaning revolves around these notions: “pressure, going downward, suppress, oppress, exert pressure upon something, diminish and overpower”. Arlo Griffiths and Eko Bastiawan in their provisional translation of Kamalagyan translate this term as “to reduce”. The same understanding is applied here.
1r8 The rare word <i>kayā</i> which recurs in the next line might be associated to a general term that could accommodate the <i>tampah</i> unit and <i>prāṇa</i> unit. The first unit refers to a measurement of land, and the second is to count living beings. In OJED, we have <i>kāya</i> signifying body and effort. But this seems not so helpful to understand the meaning of this word in the given context. In the <i>Javaansch-Nederlandsch Handwoordenboek</i> by Gericke and Roorda (1901), among the list of meanings for <i>kaya</i>, there are merit (<i>verdienste</i>) and means of maintenance (<i>middelen tot onderhoud</i>). However, it can also be understood as income too.

1r10 The name of Daksa’s chief spouse, alias the queen (<i>śrī parameśvarī</i>), <i>dyah</i> Buntir is not disclosed elsewhere but here. However, there are two mentions of the queen in this corpus. Cf. Tihang (1v2) which mentions the religious foundation <i>dharmma śrī parameśvarī I saliṅsiṅan</i>. See also Sugih Manek (A30) <i>rakryān· bini haji parameśvarī</i>. Both mentions might refer to the same person, <i>dyah</i> Buntir, as they apply the same expression <i>śrī parameśvarī</i>.
1r12 This <i>baleṁ</i> toponym although spelt differently from what we find in other inscriptions within this corpus, i.e. Wuru Tunggal and Wintang Mas as <i>valeṁ</i>.
1r12 From the name of <i>ḍaṅ ācārya</i>, Praṇidhānasupāta, we know that he is a Buddhist, as indicated by the term <i>praṇidhāna</i> which often figures specifically in the Buddhist context.
1v5 Elsewhere in this corpus, the Vuga village is found under the district of Pəar, cf. Kiringan (1r2). In Lintakan (2r2), this village appears in the same district as Kiringan.
1v6 This rare function of <i>amasaṅakan</i> is found in Rongkab (1r9) and Kanuruhan (line 11). In other context where it is followed by <i>I,</i> hence <i>amasaṅakan I</i>, cf. Palepangan (1v2) and Alasantan (3r2), that expression possibly has to be understood differently. In the prepared edition for Palepangan, Griffiths proposes a translation of <i>amasaṅakan I</i> as ‘teaming up’, followed by a question mark.
1v9 The meaning of <i>tutugan taṇḍa</i> is still not clear. But in most case, it is often complemented with the <i>-niṁ</i>, hence we’ll find it as <i>tutuganniṁ taṇḍa,</i> which still does not alter our current understanding of this term.
1v6 The term <i>uṇḍi</i> is not common to be found in the context of confirmatory gift. Its meaning on OJED is not clean either: ball with a question mark. I personally tend to envisage it like an ore, whose shape is also round like a small ball.
1v7 <i>kuluvar</i> is not found elsewhere.
1v7 <i>kuṇḍival</i> is not found elsewhere.
1v8 The village of Virəhnya, district Tilimpik, also figures in Lintakan (2r4) but is rendered there as Vərəhnya.
