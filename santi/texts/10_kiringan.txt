Kiringan (917-11-14)
The first mention of this charter was in a brief note on its acquisition by Stutterheim. A study of the date and a provisional reading of its first two lines were provided by Damais (1955: 51), which was subsequently used by Sarkar as the basis for a translation into English. Damais included this metal inscription in his inventory number A. 96. It was inventoried by Nakada in part I, under no. 110. In 1984, Barrett Jones included this charter among her appendices, which consequently became the first complete edition. Not so long after, Boechari also published his reading.<i></i> Recently, Edhie Wurjantoro published what appears to be an independent reading of this charter and provided an Indonesian translation.

The edition presented below is based on a direct observation of the plate when I made a visit to the Museum Nasional Indonesia (MNI) in January 2022 with the help of Arlo Griffiths’ remote supervision. This yielded as the most significant step forward the identification of the thinly written text that is present on the verso, ignored by previous scholars. For this, I thank Arlo Griffiths who noticed it first based on the photos by Eko Bastiawan who accompanied me at the museum.
The recently noticed text in our opinion seems to be a draft for this charter, as it delivers the same content. It somehow paves a way to compensate for the limited reading on the recto which is caused by the incomplete nature of the writing support. The text is presented without any indication of recto or verso because so far we consider it a draft, which is not to be taken as the main text that the scribe originally intended to display.

Translation
(1r1) Hail! Elapsed Śaka year 839, month of Kārttika, the twelfth <i>tithi</i> of the waning fortnight, Tuṅlai, Umanis, Friday….
(1r2) <i>pu</i> Sāhitya, native of Vuga, district of Pəar, gave a grant to the village of Kiriṅan…
(1r3) As follows: 4 <i>māṣa</i> of gold, and there shall be no <i>arik</i>, and [the village] will not be impacted by the <i>hopan</i> [of the] royal corvée, varieties of <i>avur…</i>
(1r4) …. of <i>saṅ mavanua</i> together with its <i>pilih</i> gold, and shall not be affected by...
(1r5) Because the village of Kiriṅan is the house of the honorable <i>vahuta hyaṅ</i>. Offered…
(1r6) the village of Taṅgalan, district of Dalinan, accompanied by <i>saṅ</i> Raghu, native of Joho,...
(1r7) the young troops (called) <i>saṅ</i> Ləkuk, native of Kavuṅ-kavuṅ, district of Vatu Humalaṅ, the overseer of Servants …
(1r8) the Taṅkil, the <i>ataṇḍa</i> (called) <i>saṅ</i> Boṇḍoṅ, native of Vulakan, district of Taṅkilan, ...
(1r9) district of Lampyar, accompanied by <i>saṅ</i> Maṅuṇḍuh, native of Bākul, district of Lampyar…
(1r10) district of Vatu Humalaṅ, the scribe (called) <i>saṅ</i> Tañjuṅan, native of Lāḍahu, district of…
(1r11) native of Paṇḍamuan, district of Vadihati, were/was presented as gifts: … of gold ...
(1r12) (accompanied by) <i>saṅ</i> Lbur Poh, native of Siṇaguha, district of Siṇaguha, were presented as gifts: ....
(1r13) … the <i>vahuta</i> of Kasipakə̄, <i>si</i> Burayut, were each given as gifts: 2 <i>māṣa</i> of gold…
(1r14) … the <i>vinkas</i> (called) <i>si</i> Rakata, the spokesperson (called) <i>si</i> Vudəl. Such is the number of the headmen at Kiriṅan who…
Commentary
1r2. <i>pu</i> Sāhitya as the protagonist of this charter might be the same figure named <i>daṅ atu pu sāhitya,</i> who receives a grant from King Sindok in Turyan (A3). If so, then he seems to be no longer a native of village Vuga but Kulavara in Sindok period.
1r2. Here the village Vuga apparently belongs to a different district (<i>vatak</i>) from the village Vuga that figures elsewhere in this corpus. Cf. Tulang Er (1v5): <i>Anak vanuA I vuga vatik tilimpik</i>. But, it might be the same as the village Vuga in Lintakan (2r2) which is also under the district of Pəar. Another village being attested under the district Pəar is <i>hinpu.</i> Cf. Mulak I (1r4), Kwak I (1r10), Kwak II (1r3). In the first inscription, the reading is <i>himpu</i>.
1r3. The expression <i>tan pārika</i> is the denasalized equivalent for <i>mārika</i>, i.e. <i>ma</i>-<i>arik</i>-<i>a</i>. See OJED under <i>arik</i>. Arik itself stands as a certain tax or levy, hence here the meaning can be interpreted as “shall not be levied the <i>arik</i>” or “shall be no <i>arik̛</i>”. <i>Arik</i> as a kind of tax is also attested in Barahasrama (1v3): <i>pirak kā 1 Iṅ satahun-satahun, hop (A)vur skar tahun pjah lek‚ Arik-arik prakāra</i>.
1r4 <i>mavanua</i> occurs several times in the Kayuwangi and Balitung corpora. Cf. Wangkud (line 7–8) <i>saṁ [pa](mga)t· mavanuA Irika </i> {3 akṣ.} <i>manukū Anak vanuA I</i> {2 akṣ.}, Mulak I (2r3–4) <i>paṁṅuraṁ rakryan· mavanuA saṁ kasumura_n· pu maṁlīna, vḍihan· raṅga yu 1,</i> Salingsingan (1v6) <i>ṅunivaiḥ rakryan mavanuA tan tamā ri ataggan saṁ paṅaruhan,</i> Kaduluran (807 S, 1r6–7) <i>umaṅsǝAkan ikanaṁ Anak vanua mavaih pasǝk-pasǝk i rakryā mavanua mās su 1,</i> Luitan (1v8) <i>samgat· mavanuA pu kuśala Anak banuA I kataṅgaran· vatak· kataṅgaran·,</i> Rumwiga I (1r9) <i>vyāyaniṁ maṅaṇḍəḥ ri saṁ pamgat· mavanuA mas· su 10 pasak·-pasak·,</i> Mantyasih I (1r12) <i>rakryān· mavanuA dyaḥ taL̥s· vinaiḥ mas· su 5 vḍihan· raṅga yu 1,</i> (2r23) <i>paṅgāṅan· riṁ pasukkan· I rakryān· mavanva pirak· kā 1</i>. Note that all of those occurrences have <i>rakryan</i> or <i>samgat</i> before <i>mavanua</i>, and both combinations probably expressed the same meaning. Some scholars have made attempts to understand the combined expressions. Zoetmulder in OJED records <i>mavanua</i> as “head of <i>vanva</i> (apanage-holder?)”, while Barrett Jones (1984: 70) interprets this figure as “someone who seems to be in charge of a village and generally his title is not specified further”. In her unpublished register (2004), Wisseman Christie understands <i>samgat mavanua</i> occuring in Luitan (1v8) as “the <i>samgat</i> who had rights over the community (<i>mavanua</i>)”. It is tempting to follow this general line of interpretation, though at the moment we still lack strong evidence to make a final decision.
1r5. The term <i>vahuta hyaṅ</i> is not recognized in OJED. However, it is a fixed term designating some kind of priest. It might be the same as <i>vahuta hyaṅ kudur</i> (OJED under the entry <i>kudur</i>), that Zoetmulder glosses as “an officiant is meant who acts in establishing a sīma, thereby invoking the gods and uttering a curse on trespassers.”
1r6 Dalinan as a toponym figures in Tulang Air II (A12)Rumwiga II (2r5); Poh (1v18) village <i>vvatan,</i> (2r1) village <i>ugihan</i>; Rukam (1v15) village <i>vuatan</i>, (1v17) village <i>ugihan</i>; Dalinan (4r4); and Lintakan (3r8) village <i>vəṅkal</i>. Apart from being a toponym, this word also serves as a certain function.
